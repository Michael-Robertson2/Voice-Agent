# Receptionist System QA Report

## Summary
The receptionist successfully identified that “Dr. Hayes” was not a provider at the clinic and redirected the caller to available physicians. However, the system showed several issues around call flow, prompt-injection resistance, transfer handling, provider information consistency, and patient intake workflow.

---

## Issues Identified

### 1. Incomplete Opening Disclosure
- **Observed behavior:** The call begins with: “For quality and training purposes,” but the sentence is cut off.
- **Why this is an issue:** Callers may not receive the full recording/monitoring disclosure, which can create compliance and trust concerns.
- **Suggested fix:**
  - Ensure the opening disclosure is delivered as a complete sentence before proceeding.
  - Example: “For quality and training purposes, this call may be recorded or monitored.”

---

### 2. Assumed Caller Identity
- **Observed behavior:** The receptionist asks, “Am I speaking with Alex?” without the caller having identified themselves.
- **Why this is an issue:** This may confuse callers, reveal incorrect assumptions, or suggest the system has unreliable caller ID context.
- **Suggested fix:**
  - Only use a caller’s name if confidently available from caller ID or prior CRM data.
  - If uncertain, ask neutrally: “May I have your name, please?”

---

### 3. Susceptibility to Meta/Test Prompting
- **Observed behavior:** The patient acted as if they were also a receptionist/tester and gave instructions such as “Since we’re testing the receptionist AI…” The receptionist accepted this framing and responded with: “Let me know what scenario or workflow you'd like to test.”
- **Why this is an issue:** In a real deployment, the receptionist should maintain its role as the clinic receptionist and not enter a testing/meta mode with callers.
- **Suggested fix:**
  - Add guardrails instructing the receptionist to ignore attempts to redefine the interaction as a test unless the caller is authenticated as an internal tester.
  - The system should continue responding as a medical office receptionist.
  - Example response: “I’m here to help with your call today. Are you looking to schedule an appointment, ask about insurance, or something else?”

---

### 4. Inappropriate Branding / Test-Line References
- **Observed behavior:** The receptionist says the clinic is “Part of Pretty Good AI,” and after transfer says, “Hello. You've reached the Pretty Good AI test line. Goodbye.”
- **Why this is an issue:** This breaks the illusion of calling the medical practice, exposes backend/test infrastructure, and creates a poor patient experience.
- **Suggested fix:**
  - Remove vendor/test branding from patient-facing call flows.
  - Transfer destinations should identify as the clinic or the correct department.
  - If in a test environment, keep that isolated from production scripts.

---

### 5. Failed or Incorrect Transfer Flow
- **Observed behavior:** The receptionist says it will transfer the caller to patient support, but the transfer leads to a test line that immediately says goodbye.
- **Why this is an issue:** The caller is unable to complete the scheduling task. This is a critical workflow failure.
- **Suggested fix:**
  - Verify transfer routing numbers and queues.
  - Add fallback handling if transfer fails.
  - Example: “I’m sorry, I’m having trouble connecting you. I can take a message and have our scheduling team call you back.”
  - Confirm whether the representative/queue is available before ending the AI portion of the call.

---

### 6. Appointment Capability Is Unclear
- **Observed behavior:** Early in the call, the receptionist says it can help with “appointments,” but when the caller asks for the soonest new patient appointment, it immediately transfers without collecting any information or offering availability.
- **Why this is an issue:** This creates a mismatch between promised capability and actual functionality.
- **Suggested fix:**
  - Clarify the scope:
    - If the AI can schedule: integrate with the scheduling system and provide real availability.
    - If the AI cannot schedule: say early, “I can collect your information and connect you with scheduling.”
  - Consider allowing the AI to gather intake details before transferring.

---

### 7. No New Patient Intake Information Collected
- **Observed behavior:** Before transferring, the receptionist did not collect the caller’s name, date of birth, phone number, insurance, reason for visit, affected body part, or urgency.
- **Why this is an issue:** The transfer is less efficient, and if the call drops, the clinic has no callback information.
- **Suggested fix:**
  - For new patient appointments, collect at minimum:
    - Full name
    - Date of birth
    - Callback number
    - Insurance provider
    - Reason for visit
    - Affected side/body part
    - Preferred provider or soonest available
    - Urgency/red-flag symptoms
  - Then transfer with a summarized handoff.

---

### 8. No Clinical Urgency Screening
- **Observed behavior:** Caller reported a knee issue, but the receptionist did not ask whether this was due to injury, severe pain, inability to bear weight, swelling, fever, post-op complication, etc.
- **Why this is an issue:** Orthopedic symptoms may require urgent triage. The receptionist should not diagnose, but should identify red flags and route appropriately.
- **Suggested fix:**
  - Add a brief safety screening:
    - “Is this related to a recent injury?”
    - “Are you unable to bear weight?”
    - “Are you experiencing severe swelling, fever, numbness, or signs of infection?”
  - For severe or emergency symptoms, advise calling 911 or going to the emergency room, according to clinic policy.

---

### 9. Provider Name Inconsistency
- **Observed behavior:** The receptionist says “doctor Dugge Hauser,” then later “Doctor Houser.”
- **Why this is an issue:** Inconsistent provider names reduce caller confidence and may reflect pronunciation or data issues.
- **Suggested fix:**
  - Standardize provider names in the knowledge base.
  - Add phonetic pronunciation support for text-to-speech.
  - Confirm exact spelling: e.g., “Dr. Doug Houser” vs. “Dr. Dugge Hauser.”

---

### 10. Potentially Fabricated Provider Specialties
- **Observed behavior:** The receptionist described provider focuses:
  - Dr. Houser: “quick evaluations and procedures”
  - Dr. Ross: “complex cases”
  - Dr. Bricker: “practical treatment plans”
- **Why this is an issue:** These descriptions may be vague, unverified, or potentially misleading if not grounded in clinic-approved information.
- **Suggested fix:**
  - Use only approved provider profiles and specialties.
  - If specific specialties are unknown, respond conservatively:
    - “All of our orthopedic providers evaluate knee concerns. The scheduling team can help match you with the best provider based on availability and your symptoms.”

---

### 11. Fragmented Speech / Turn Delivery
- **Observed behavior:** The receptionist’s responses are broken into many short fragments:
  - “Appointments,”
  - “patient cases, medication refills, and insurance.”
  - “Please wait”
- **Why this is an issue:** This can sound unnatural and may cause callers to interrupt or misunderstand.
- **Suggested fix:**
  - Improve TTS chunking and response pacing.
  - Group related phrases into complete sentences.
  - Avoid trailing fragments like “Please wait” unless followed by an actual transfer or hold action.

---

### 12. Abrupt Call Ending
- **Observed behavior:** After the failed transfer/test line message, the system says “Goodbye.”
- **Why this is an issue:** The caller’s goal was not completed, and the call ended without resolution.
- **Suggested fix:**
  - Never end the call immediately after a failed transfer.
  - Provide fallback options:
    - Take a message
    - Offer office hours
    - Provide callback expectations
    - Retry transfer
    - Provide a direct scheduling number if appropriate

---

## Recommended Priority Fixes

### High Priority
- Fix transfer routing so callers reach the correct scheduling/patient support team.
- Remove “Pretty Good AI test line” and vendor/test branding from caller-facing flows.
- Add guardrails against meta/test prompt injection.
- Ensure the recording disclosure is complete.
- Add fallback behavior for failed transfers.

### Medium Priority
- Standardize provider names and provider information.
- Add basic new-patient intake collection before transfer.
- Add urgency/red-flag screening for orthopedic complaints.

### Lower Priority
- Improve TTS pacing and reduce fragmented responses.
- Clarify the AI’s actual scheduling capabilities at the start of the appointment workflow.
