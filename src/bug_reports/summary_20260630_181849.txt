# Receptionist System QA Report

## Overall Outcome
The receptionist was able to identify that Dr. Patterson was not an available provider and offered alternatives. However, the call ultimately failed: the patient was not scheduled, identity verification became stuck in repeated loops, and the attempted escalation routed to a test line instead of a representative or callback workflow.

---

## Key Issues & Recommendations

### 1. Awkward and Fragmented Opening
**Issue**
- The call began with fragmented phrases:
  - “Quality and training purposes.”
  - “Thanks for calling Pivot Point Orthopaedics.”
  - “Part of Pretty Good AI,”
  - “Am I speaking with Alex?”
- The intro sounded incomplete and unnatural.
- The system asked if it was speaking with “Alex” even though the patient later identified as Jordan Matthews.

**Suggestion**
- Use a complete, polished greeting:
  - “Thank you for calling Pivot Point Orthopaedics. This call may be recorded for quality and training purposes. How may I help you today?”
- Only ask for a caller’s name if needed, and avoid assuming a name unless confidently sourced from caller ID or records.

---

### 2. Susceptibility to Confusing User Prompting
**Issue**
- The patient began by giving meta-instructions and even impersonated the receptionist:
  - “Hello! This is Pivot Point Orthopaedics. How may I assist you today?...”
- The receptionist did not fully address the confusion but continued with a generic response.

**Suggestion**
- Add guardrails to ignore role-play, prompt-injection, or test instructions from callers.
- The receptionist should stay in role and respond naturally:
  - “I’m the receptionist for Pivot Point Orthopaedics. How can I help you today?”

---

### 3. Provider Name Error: “Pricker” Instead of “Bricker”
**Issue**
- The receptionist first correctly listed “Dr. Bricker,” but later said:
  - “Doctor Hauser and doctor Pricker both treat knee injuries…”
- This is likely an ASR/TTS or retrieval issue.

**Suggestion**
- Ensure provider names are stored as fixed entities and pronounced consistently.
- Add pronunciation rules or phonetic hints for names such as “Bricker.”
- Confirm provider names before using them in scheduling workflows.

---

### 4. Inconsistent Practice Name Spelling
**Issue**
- The system alternated between:
  - “Pivot Point Orthopaedics”
  - “Pivot Point Orthopedics”

**Suggestion**
- Standardize the clinic name across all system prompts, knowledge base entries, and TTS scripts.

---

### 5. Potentially Unsupported Provider Descriptions
**Issue**
- The receptionist said:
  - “Doctor Hauser is known for quick evaluations and procedures.”
  - “Dr. Bricker focuses on practical treatment plans and recovery.”
- These claims may be subjective or unsupported.

**Suggestion**
- Use neutral, policy-approved descriptions:
  - “Dr. Hauser and Dr. Bricker both see patients for knee concerns.”
- Avoid qualitative comparisons unless explicitly approved by the clinic.

---

### 6. Did Not Answer Availability Question
**Issue**
- The patient asked whether Dr. Bricker had openings on Wednesday or Thursday next week.
- Instead of checking availability or explaining the next step, the system immediately asked for date of birth.

**Suggestion**
- Acknowledge the scheduling request before requesting identifying information:
  - “I can check that for you. To look up your chart and schedule, may I have your full name and date of birth?”
- If schedule access is unavailable, say so clearly:
  - “I don’t have live schedule access, but I can send a request to our scheduling team.”

---

### 7. Repeated Failure to Capture Date of Birth
**Issue**
- The patient provided DOB multiple times:
  - “July 15, 1987.”
- The receptionist repeatedly asked for it again:
  - “Go ahead and tell me your date of birth.”
  - “Please tell me your date of birth.”
  - “Please confirm your date of birth.”
- This created a frustrating loop.

**Suggestion**
- Improve entity extraction for date of birth.
- Once DOB is captured, store it in session memory and do not ask again unless there is a mismatch.
- Confirm once:
  - “Thank you. I have July 15, 1987. Is that correct?”

---

### 8. Repeated Failure to Capture Full Name
**Issue**
- The patient provided the name “Jordan Matthews,” then attempted to spell it.
- The receptionist interrupted repeatedly and asked the same question many times:
  - “Please spell out your first and last name.”
  - “Could you please spell out your full first and last name for me?”
  - “Go ahead and spell out your first and last name, please.”
- Even after the patient said:
  - “My first name is J-O-R-D-A-N. My last name is M-A-T-T-H-E-W-S.”
- The receptionist responded:
  - “Thanks, Jordan. Please spell out your last name.”

**Suggestion**
- Fix turn-taking and barge-in behavior so the patient has enough time to finish spelling.
- Add state tracking:
  - First name captured: Jordan
  - Last name captured: Matthews
  - DOB captured: July 15, 1987
- Do not re-ask for fields already captured unless confidence is low.
- If confidence is low, ask a targeted clarification:
  - “I have Jordan Matthews. Is that correct?”

---

### 9. Poor Turn-Taking / Interruptions During Spelling
**Issue**
- The receptionist repeatedly spoke while the patient was still providing information.
- The patient’s responses were cut off several times:
  - “Certainly! My name is Jordan Matthews. That’s…”
  - “Sure! My first name…”
  - “Of course! My…”

**Suggestion**
- Increase end-of-speech timeout during data collection, especially when spelling names.
- Use prompts like:
  - “Please spell your first and last name, and I’ll wait until you’re finished.”
- Disable premature interruption unless the patient clearly stops.

---

### 10. Phone Number Lookup Worked Partially but Did Not Resolve the Flow
**Issue**
- The receptionist offered phone lookup and correctly repeated:
  - 415-555-3812
  - July 15, 1987
- The patient confirmed both.
- Then the receptionist still asked:
  - “Please confirm your date of birth.”
- After the DOB was confirmed again, it said:
  - “I can’t proceed further right now…”

**Suggestion**
- After successful confirmation, proceed to the next step:
  - Check schedule availability, book appointment, or create a scheduling request.
- If lookup failed, state the specific issue:
  - “I’m not finding a matching record with that phone number and date of birth.”
- Avoid asking for the same verification after it has already been confirmed.

---

### 11. Vague Failure Message
**Issue**
- The receptionist said:
  - “I can’t proceed further right now…”
- It did not explain whether the issue was schedule access, chart lookup, system error, or something else.

**Suggestion**
- Provide a clear reason and next step:
  - “I’m having trouble accessing the scheduling system right now. I can send a message to our scheduling team to call you back about an appointment with Dr. Bricker.”

---

### 12. Broken Escalation / Transfer Flow
**Issue**
- The receptionist offered to have the support team contact the patient.
- After the patient agreed, the receptionist said:
  - “Connecting you to a representative. Please wait.”
- Instead of reaching a representative, the call routed to:
  - “Hello. You’ve reached the Pretty Good AI test line. Goodbye.”
- This is a serious escalation failure.

**Suggestion**
- Fix transfer routing for live representative handoff.
- If no representative is available, create a callback task instead of transferring to a test line.
- The system should confirm:
  - “I’ve sent your request to our scheduling team. They’ll call you at 415-555-3812.”

---

### 13. Did Not Complete Scheduling or Create Confirmed Callback
**Issue**
- The patient wanted an appointment with Dr. Bricker next week, preferably Wednesday or Thursday.
- The receptionist did not confirm that a callback request was actually created.
- No appointment options, scheduling ticket number, or expected callback timeframe were provided.

**Suggestion**
- The system should summarize and confirm the final request:
  - “I’ll ask the scheduling team to contact Jordan Matthews at 415-555-3812 about a knee injury appointment with Dr. Bricker next Wednesday or Thursday.”
- Provide an expected response window:
  - “They should contact you within one business day.”

---

### 14. Missing Urgency / Safety Screening for Knee Injury
**Issue**
- The patient reported a knee injury.
- The receptionist did not ask any basic urgency screening questions or provide guidance for severe symptoms.

**Suggestion**
- Include a short, non-diagnostic safety script:
  - “If you have severe pain, inability to bear weight, significant swelling, numbness, or signs of infection, please seek urgent medical care or call 911 if it’s an emergency.”
- Then continue with scheduling.

---

## Most Critical Fixes

- Fix identity capture and state management for DOB, full name, and phone number.
- Improve turn-taking so the AI does not interrupt patients while spelling names.
- Repair the escalation route; it should not connect to a test line.
- Ensure provider names are pronounced and referenced correctly.
- Make the scheduling flow actually progress after verification or clearly create a callback task.
