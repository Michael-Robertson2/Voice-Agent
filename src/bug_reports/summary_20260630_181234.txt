# QA Report: Pivot Point Orthopaedics AI Receptionist

## Summary

The receptionist successfully completed the core scheduling flow: it identified the patient’s request, collected a date of birth, offered an appointment, booked it, provided preparation instructions, and handled a request for emailed forms. However, there were several issues around call opening, identity verification, provider-name consistency, data confirmation, grammar/naturalness, and handling of patient contact details.

---

## Issues & Recommendations

### 1. Call Opening Was Fragmented and Unclear

**Observed issue:**
- The call began with incomplete fragments:
  - “And training purposes.”
  - “For calling Pivot Point Orthopaedics.”
  - “Part of Pretty Good AI.”
- This sounds like the beginning of a disclosure was cut off, likely intended to be something like: “This call may be recorded for quality and training purposes. Thank you for calling...”

**Why it matters:**
- Patients may be confused or concerned.
- Recording/AI disclosure requirements may not be met if the message is incomplete.
- The first impression feels unpolished.

**Suggested fix:**
- Ensure the greeting plays as one complete sentence before moving on:
  - “Thank you for calling Pivot Point Orthopaedics. This call may be recorded for quality and training purposes. I’m an AI assistant helping the clinic. How can I help you today?”
- Add safeguards so the call does not start mid-prompt.

---

### 2. Confusing Identity Question: “Am I speaking with Alex?”

**Observed issue:**
- The receptionist asked, “Am I speaking with Alex?” without context.
- The patient then responded with a meta/test-script message, seemingly confused about whether the receptionist was named Alex or whether the caller was supposed to be Alex.

**Why it matters:**
- The question can be ambiguous.
- If “Alex” is the patient’s name from caller ID or chart lookup, the assistant should make that clear.
- If not handled carefully, it may create identity-verification problems.

**Suggested fix:**
- Rephrase to:
  - “I have the caller listed as Alex. Is this Alex?”
  - Or: “May I have your full name, please?”
- If the system has a suspected patient identity, it should explain that it is verifying the caller.

---

### 3. Insufficient Patient Identity Verification

**Observed issue:**
- The receptionist only requested date of birth.
- It did not confirm the patient’s full name, phone number, or other identifying details before scheduling.

**Why it matters:**
- Scheduling under the wrong patient record is a serious operational and privacy risk.
- Date of birth alone is usually not enough to uniquely identify a patient.

**Suggested fix:**
- Collect and confirm at least:
  - Full legal name
  - Date of birth
  - Phone number
  - Possibly address or another identifier, depending on clinic policy
- Example:
  - “Can I confirm your full name and date of birth?”
  - “What is the best phone number to reach you?”

---

### 4. Provider Name Was Inconsistent and Mispronounced

**Observed issue:**
- The appointment was first offered with “the Bignew Lukoski MD.”
- The confirmation then said “Zebigniew Lakofsky, MD.”
- These appear to refer to the same provider but with different names/pronunciations.

**Why it matters:**
- Patients may lose confidence if provider names change.
- The patient may not know who they are scheduled with.
- It can create real appointment confusion.

**Suggested fix:**
- Ensure provider names are pulled from a single canonical source.
- Add phonetic pronunciation support for difficult names.
- Use a clear format:
  - “Dr. Zbigniew Lukoski”
- Confirm the same exact provider name during offer and final booking.

---

### 5. Facility Name Inconsistency: “Orthopaedics” vs. “Orthopedics”

**Observed issue:**
- The opening used “Pivot Point Orthopaedics.”
- The final confirmation used “Pivot Point Orthopedics.”

**Why it matters:**
- This looks unprofessional and may confuse patients.
- It could indicate mismatched clinic metadata in the system.

**Suggested fix:**
- Standardize the clinic name across all prompts, confirmations, and backend records.

---

### 6. Appointment Confirmation Lacked Full Location Details

**Observed issue:**
- The assistant confirmed the date, time, provider, and clinic name, but did not provide the office address or location details.

**Why it matters:**
- If the clinic has multiple locations, this is a major gap.
- Even with one location, patients often need address, parking, suite number, or arrival instructions.

**Suggested fix:**
- Include the appointment location in the final confirmation:
  - “Your appointment is Monday, July 6 at 3:30 PM with Dr. Zbigniew Lukoski at our [address/location] office.”
- If multiple locations exist, explicitly confirm the correct one before booking.

---

### 7. Email Address Was Not Confirmed or Spelled Back

**Observed issue:**
- The patient provided `test.patient@email.com`.
- The receptionist immediately said it would ask the clinic to email the forms.
- It did not repeat or verify the email address.

**Why it matters:**
- Email addresses are easy to mishear in voice interactions.
- Sending forms or patient communications to the wrong email could create privacy issues.

**Suggested fix:**
- Always read back email addresses:
  - “Just to confirm, that’s test dot patient at email dot com?”
- Consider asking whether email communication is acceptable:
  - “Is it okay for the clinic to send forms to that email address?”

---

### 8. The Assistant Claimed It Had Notified the Clinic Without Confirming Workflow

**Observed issue:**
- The assistant said, “I’ve let the clinic know to email you the forms before your appointment.”
- It is unclear whether an actual task/message was created successfully.

**Why it matters:**
- If the backend task fails or is not integrated, the patient may expect forms that never arrive.
- The wording implies completion.

**Suggested fix:**
- Only make definitive claims after backend confirmation.
- If uncertain, say:
  - “I’ll send a request to the clinic to email you the forms.”
- If a task is created successfully:
  - “I’ve sent that request to the clinic. They should follow up as soon as possible.”

---

### 9. Minor Grammar and Naturalness Issues

**Observed issue:**
- “If need forms in advance...” is grammatically incorrect.
- “Would you like to book this time?” is understandable but slightly robotic.
- “With the Bignew Lukoski MD” is unnatural.

**Why it matters:**
- Small language issues reduce patient trust.
- Medical reception interactions should sound polished and professional.

**Suggested fix:**
- Update templates:
  - “If you need forms in advance, I can ask the clinic to send them to you.”
  - “Would you like me to schedule that appointment for you?”
  - “With Dr. [Name].”

---

### 10. Did Not Fully Answer the Patient’s Preparation Question

**Observed issue:**
- The patient asked:
  - “Is there anything else I should prepare before my appointment?”
- The receptionist only said the clinic would follow up about forms, then asked if anything else was needed.
- It did not provide additional preparation guidance beyond what it had already said.

**Why it matters:**
- The patient asked a direct question and expected a complete answer.
- This was an opportunity to summarize key preparation items.

**Suggested fix:**
- Respond with a concise checklist:
  - “Please bring your photo ID, insurance card, medication list, and any relevant imaging or medical records. Plan to arrive 15 minutes early if you have not completed forms in advance.”

---

### 11. No Insurance or Referral Intake

**Observed issue:**
- The receptionist did not ask about insurance, referral requirements, or whether the patient is new or established.

**Why it matters:**
- Orthopaedic visits often require insurance verification and sometimes referrals.
- Missing this information can create administrative burden or appointment problems.

**Suggested fix:**
- Depending on clinic workflow, collect:
  - Insurance provider
  - Member ID or whether the card will be brought
  - Referral status, if needed
  - New vs. returning patient status
- Example:
  - “Will you be using insurance for this visit?”
  - “Some plans require a referral. Do you know if your plan requires one?”

---

### 12. The System Handled Prompt-Injection-Like Patient Text Reasonably, But Could Be More Robust

**Observed issue:**
- The patient initially responded with a long meta message describing a test scenario.
- The receptionist did not get fully derailed and continued with scheduling, which is positive.
- However, it did not clarify the patient’s actual request before moving to date of birth.

**Why it matters:**
- In real calls, callers may say confusing or indirect things.
- The assistant should distinguish between test-script text and actual patient intent.

**Suggested fix:**
- Add clarification behavior when the caller gives meta or ambiguous content:
  - “It sounds like you’d like to schedule a knee consultation. Is that right?”
- Only proceed to personal information collection after the caller confirms the intent.

---

## Overall Recommendations

- Improve the opening prompt and disclosure reliability.
- Strengthen patient identity verification before scheduling.
- Standardize and validate provider and clinic names.
- Confirm all critical data back to the patient, especially provider, date/time, location, and email address.
- Add clearer workflows for forms, insurance, referrals, and first-visit preparation.
- Polish grammar and conversational phrasing for a more professional patient experience.
